Blast Radius

Not just what broke — who it breaks. Consumers declare what they use; SpecVault names the teams actually at risk.

~4:00
estimated duration
Target: consumer teams, API producers, platform engineers
Scene 1 — The "page everyone" problem0:00 – 0:30

On Screen

A service with 8 subscribers listed. A breaking change fires. All 8 receive the same alert email. Pan to an inbox flooded with alerts — most greyed out as irrelevant.

Voiceover

"When a breaking change fires, most alert systems page every subscriber. But most subscribers aren't actually affected — they call a different endpoint, or read a different field. Alert everyone, and they learn to ignore you. SpecVault knows the difference."

Scene 2 — Declaring usage0:30 – 1:15

On Screen

Open a service's Subscribers tab. Show the "Declare Endpoints" panel: method dropdown (GET), path field (/users/{id}), optional field (email). Submit. Row appears: payments@acme — GET /users/{id} — email. A second subscriber adds GET /orders with no field.

Voiceover

"Each subscriber can declare the endpoints — and the specific fields — their integration actually uses. The payments team reads email off GET /users. The orders team calls GET /orders entirely. Both declarations are stored against their subscription — this is the data that drives blast-radius attribution."

Scene 3 — A breaking change lands1:15 – 2:00

On Screen

Publish a new spec version. Jump to the Changes tab — a new change event: 2 breaking. Expand the event. Two rows: "response field 200.email removed — GET /users/{id}" and "required field added — POST /orders".

Voiceover

"A new version lands with two breaking changes: a response field removed on GET /users, and a new required field on POST /orders. Now SpecVault doesn't just surface these — it asks: which consumers declared usage that intersects exactly these?"

Scene 4 — Attribution: who gets flagged, who doesn't2:00 – 2:55

On Screen

On the change event, expand the Blast radius section. Row 1 (email removed from GET /users): impacts payments@acme — uses GET /users/{id}, field email. Row 2 (required field on POST /orders): impacts orders@acme — uses GET /orders, then impact unknown — 3 subscribers (no declaration). payments@acme spared on row 2.

Voiceover

"The removed email field flags payments directly — they declared it. The new required field on POST /orders flags orders@acme, but payments is spared because their declaration is on a read endpoint, not the orders write path. Three other subscribers haven't declared anything — so they show as 'impact unknown', not safe. SpecVault never silently assumes a consumer is clear."

Scene 5 — Field-level precision2:55 – 3:35

On Screen

Same endpoint GET /users/{id}, two subscribers. analytics@acme declared field created_at; payments@acme declared email. A change removes email. Blast radius: payments flagged, analytics spared. Annotate: "Same endpoint, different field, different fate."

Voiceover

"Field-level declarations give you per-field precision, even when two teams call the same endpoint. Analytics reads created_at — the email removal doesn't touch them. Payments reads email — they get the alert. The signal is exactly as narrow as the change."

Scene 6 — CTA3:35 – 4:00

On Screen

Recap chips: Declare endpoints · Declare fields · Breaking change lands · Impacted named · Unknown surfaced. URL: specvault.sentinelflux.in

Voiceover

"Declare what you depend on. When something breaks, SpecVault names you — not everyone on the list. That's the difference between an alert you act on and noise you mute. specvault.sentinelflux.in."
